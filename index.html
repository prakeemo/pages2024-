A girl I saw on a bus.
She reminded me of ribbons and nails.
Both meant to fix things in place.
A nail needs a hammer,
a ribbon, a hand to tie it.
We ordered delivery in Thailand, waiting together in the lobby.
I think I smoked more there than anywhere else—
Marlboro Gold, 150 baht at the convenience store,
about 5,500 won in Korean money.
I went to School for a moment today.
Arriving near lunchtime,
most of the students were eating in the park.
Sue and I bought some food from the market
and sat in the park, too.
A strange feeling washed over me.
I think it was around 5 p.m.
I visited a university store in Thailand with Sue, and beside a fan that didn’t work, a small child was sitting.
Though the fan stayed still, its shadow carried coolness.
The sun rises from the east, and now it spills into my room.
This place is so quiet.
This morning, I woke to the sound of hoofbeats.
A small horse, maybe a foal?
The horses here never run.
The clip-clop of their steps is so precise, like a machine— a clock chiming the morning hour.
The light that has penetrated the blind is reflected in every palm joint.
The wrinkled palm lines look bright because of the sunlight.
People predict the future by looking at the palm of their hand.
I can't believe I can see a person's personality, emotional health, or even the overall flow of life just by looking at the lines on my palm...
What does it feel like to leave the future to someone?
I woke earlier than usual today.
Brushing my teeth, still half-dreaming.
I was supposed to see Sue off to her atelier, but the bed held me too tightly.
After she left, I slept two more hours.
When I was young, I loved napping,
but now it feels like stolen time.
Naps no longer refresh me;
they leave me more tired, a weight I can’t shake.
Morning sunlight is like a wave.
Today, it almost devoured my room that I hurried to pull the blinds.
The vitality of the morning can be terrifying.
Sunlight reveals everything:
dark alleys, highways,
the sky, the sea,
even the shadows on people’s faces—
it brightens graves, too.
On summer nights thick with insect songs,
I always went to the Sky Bridge.

"If I leave now, I’ll catch the sunset."

Color is something you feel, not learn.
The sky holds every shade I can imagine.
Morning, noon, and evening skies—
each one a new canvas.
As the sun moves, the sky shifts, too.

What’s on the moon

that makes people spend so much to reach it?
I was blessed by the sky today.
Thick clouds hung in the sky,
and the light was very sacred like stained glass used in medieval cathedrals.
It is said that most of the medieval Bibles were mainly paintings.
It was because people at that time had such a high illiteracy rate that they could not read the Bible in writing.
There was a huge debate between Eastern and Western churches over the use of paintings in churches.
Was it because of Moses' commandments? I don't remember exactly, but Eastern Christianity had a doctrine that required the service of a single god, so it was strictly prohibited to form and serve him.
Even the paintings in the Bible and stained glass were considered idolatry.
Then the Great Pope Gregorius came forward and advocated the use of biblical paintings, and after that he could write biblical paintings, but not all paintings were allowed.
To prevent people from turning their attention to the other side, the painting strictly focused on the essence, and eventually the high-quality depictions and representations of the Roman Empire gradually declined.
Lying on the floor this evening, I looked at the sky for a long time.

I brought an umbrella because there was a forecast that it would rain, but I could only see dark clouds and it didn't rain.
I don't like rain because my socks are wet, but I like dark clouds.
The day before I left Germany for Switzerland,
I saw a flock of birds in flight.
As a child, I loved birds,
chasing them endlessly,
though I only knew sparrows and magpies.

Looking back,
I think I loved their image more than the birds themselves.
Every animal I saw lived on the ground,
but birds belonged to the sky.
When I flew on planes,
I imagined being inside a bird.

But I stopped looking at birds with awe
after a school lesson—
learning they mostly ate insects.
Even as a child,
the sight of a bug would send me running.
Bugs were tiny hells,
1 cm of condensed dread.

Could it be that my love for birds
was tied to my fear of bugs?
Their shared sky
made one evil, the other good.
Yet, I didn’t want birds to eat bugs—
I wanted them to conquer them,
to rise above.

What does it matter now?
Tomorrow, I’m heading back to Switzerland.

火田

Fields of Fire.
The night in the industrial district
is brighter than the inside of the train.

Steam from the factories meets glaring lights,
turning even whiter.
Everything evaporates.

Moments pass by in an instant,
whether joy or sorrow—
turn around, and they’re already behind you.
Fireworks are beautiful when they burst,
but they vanish so quickly.
